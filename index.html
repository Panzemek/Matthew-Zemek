Description

I am a graduate of the University of Washington coding bootcamp, having recently earned my certificate from the program. However, I've been a logic nerd my entire life; one of the first things I did as a child when our family got its first computer was to seek out all the logic puzzles I could find online to solve for fun. In hindsight, that was probably a sign, and the fascination for deducing solutions never really left. It did take a bit to figure out that I could do so for profit as well as fun, but now that I am here I'm eager to apply those same logical problem-solving skills in a professional capacity.

My technical experience prior to the certificate program was varied and often improvised. I tended to gravitate towards (or, in some cases, be thrust into) technical roles at my various positions. Sometimes this was part of the plan, such as hiring on for the Christmas season at Nintendo when the Wii U was released as end-line network support. Sometimes it developed over time, like when a construction company start-up I was working for realized they didn't have any experience with their new accounting software and I was the only one on staff who could figure out how to use it. In still other cases it was quite simply due to panic, as was the case when all the building staff who knew how to operate the camera systems had left and I was, despite being there to temporarily fill in the gap, the only one there able to learn them before the police arrived after an assault had taken place.

In each case, the theme has remained consistent: Make it work. In some cases there was more support for doing so, and in some less - my preference is, certainly, to have a plan for how we'll get there - but improvising when things were at risk of falling apart has been a skill I've been forced to acquire. I thus have practice putting out fires, a few times literally. Whatever position I find myself in I will continue to adapt as I have all along.
